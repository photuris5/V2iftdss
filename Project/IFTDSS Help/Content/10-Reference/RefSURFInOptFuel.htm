Surface Fire Behavior Options – Fuel

Out of all these options, you can choose just one option per run.

Name Units Brief Description
Fuel Parameters (for custom fuel modeling) Tons per acre, feet2 per feet3, feet, percentage, Btus per pound; tonne per hectare (Mg/ha), meters2 per meter3, meters, percentage, kilojoules per kilograms For custom fuel modeling, individual fuel parameters are required for fuel bed depth, extinction moisture content, heat of combustion, and loading and surface area-to-volume ratio by fuel particle size class. Fuel model parameters can be initialized to those of an existing fuel model. (Burgan, 1987; Burgan and Rothermel, 1984)
Fuels Models (standard or custom) A drop-down list Standard or custom fire behavior fuel models for the surface fire spread model. Standard fire behavior fuel models are the 13 from Anderson (1982) and the 40 from Scott and Burgan (2005).
Special case fuel model, palmetto-gallberry Years, feet, percentage, feet2 per acre A special-case fire behavior fuel model for palmetto-gallberry (developed by Hough and Albini, 1978). This fuel model differs from standard and custom fuel models. The age of palmetto-gallberry rough, palmetto coverage, understory height, and overstory basal area are used to calculate the fuel parameters needed by the surface fire spread module.
Special case fuel model, western aspen A drop-down list, percentage A special-case fuel model for western aspen was developed by Brown and Simmerman (1986). This fuel model differs from standard and custom fuel models. There are five aspen fuel types: aspen/shrub, aspen/tall forb, aspen/low forb, (conifer overstory) mixed/shrub, and (conifer overstory) mixed/forb. Aspen curing level is used to calculate parameters needed by the surface fire spread module.
Two fuel models, 2-dimensional expected spread A drop-down list, percentage The recommended method for calculating rate of spread through a mixture of two fuel models. The expected spread rate through the fuel complex is estimated by examining all possible spread paths through a matrix of the fuels. The module uses the fastest fire spread rate, which is obtained by allowing the fire to burn around, rather than through, patches of slower burning fuels (Finney, 2002).
Two fuel models, area weighted A drop-down list, percentage The area weighted method for two fuel models was used in the old BEHAVE system (Andrews, 1986). This method simply weights the spread rate of each fuel model by its coverage. This approach may be appropriate if the size of the fire is small compared to the size of the fuel patches. (Rothermel, 1983)
Two fuel models, harmonic mean A drop-down list, percentage The harmonic mean method for two fuel models is applicable to situations in which the fire will burn through alternating bands of two fuel types, such as burning up an elevation gradient or through a fuel modification zone. The fire must burn through one band to reach the next, and has no opportunity to spread around the slower burning fuel. (Fujioka, 1985)
