Risk Assessment Glossary

This glossary defines terms specific to risk assessment.

Term Definition
burn probability at high flame lengths The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined high flame length class.
burn probability at low flame lengths The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined low flame length class.
burn probability at medium flame lengths The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined medium flame length class.
burn probability at very high flame lengths The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined very high flame length class.
flame length The distance from the ground at the leading edge of the flame to the tip of the flame.
flame length class Flame lengths are divided into low, medium, high, and very high lengths. These divisions are the flame length classes.
flame length probabilities approach One of the two risk assessment approaches in IFTDSS. (The other is the worst-case flame lengths approach.) This method uses the response functions developed by Calkin et. al. (2010a) and modeled flame length burn probabilities from the RANDIG burn probability simulator to estimate the likelihood of the area represented by the pixel burning, and the potential consequences if the area represented by the pixel is burned by a backing fire, a flanking fire, or a head fire.
IFT-FlamMap-RANDIG In IFTDSS, the burn probability module (IFT-FlamMap-RANDIG) is used to simulate burn probability across an entire landscape. The burn probability module creates raster maps of composite burn probabilities (burn probabilities for all flame lengths) and raster maps of burn probabilities for a set of user-defined flame length classes. The module can be used for hazard analysis and is an essential component of the risk assessment workflow.
net value change (NVC) multiplier An intermediate value used to calculate the net value change based on the response function.
overall burn probability The likelihood that the area represented by a pixel will burn given a random ignition across the area of interest (for a specific set of environmental conditions). Burn probability is not an estimate of the future likelihood of a wildfire and should not be confused with empirical wildfire occurrence probabilities.
relative net value change The relative net change in land value due to fire is assumed to be a function of fire behavior, and includes both positive and negative effects of fire.
response function The mathematical relationship between fire characteristic and fire outcome. The response functions table describes the effect of the fire on the values at risk.
user-defined flame length IFTDSS, you can define the lengths for each flame length class, or you can use a default set of lengths. When you define the lengths, those are called user-defined flame lengths. The names of the classes always remain the same; it is just the lengths assigned to each class that can change.
values at risk The features on the landscape that are influenced positively and/or negatively by fire. Also known as highly valued resources (HVR). Examples include archaeological sites, historic buildings, wildland urban interface, airports, highway buffers, and conifer forests.
worst-case flame lengths approach One of the two risk assessment approaches in IFTDSS. (The other is the flame length probabilities approach.) This approach is referred to as the “worst case” estimation of fire risk because it is based on a single FlamMap run, where the areas represented by every pixel are all always assumed to burn under the worst-case (i.e., by a head fire) conditions.
