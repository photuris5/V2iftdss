Fuels Treatment Workflow

Fuels Treatment Workflow contains options to run fire behavior, (IFT-FlamMap), minimum travel time (IFT-MTT), and risk assessments across a landscape. The fuels treatment workflow is unique in that while it contains some of the same modeling capabilities as other workflows, the Fuel Treatment workflows are set up to conduct a run across an untreated landscape, allow the user to make changes on the landscape to simulate fuel treatments, then run the same models again, thereby facilitating easy comparison of pre-treatment and post-treatment modeling output.

Calculate fire behavior across a landscape (using IFT-FlamMap)

With IFT-FlamMap, IFTDSS provides tools for calculating potential fire behavior using surface fire behavior. The spatial fire behavior outputs provide information about the distribution of possible fire behaviors across an area of interest.

Calculate minimum travel time (using MTT)

Calculate minimum travel time (using MTT)

The minimum travel time (MTT) module is a two-dimensional fire growth model. Fire growth and behavior are calculated by searching for pathways with minimum fire spread times from point, line, or polygon ignition sources.

Calculate burn probability across a landscape (using IFT-RANDIG)

Calculate burn probability across a landscape (using IFT-RANDIG)

RANDIG is a mechanistic fire spread program that uses the minimum travel time algorithms in FlamMap to simulate fire spread across landscapes using constant wind and fuel moisture inputs (Finney, 2006). RANDIG creates raster maps of composite burn probabilities (burn probabilities for all flame lengths) and raster maps of burn probabilities for a set of user-defined flame length classes.

Outputs

Name Units Brief Description
Burn Probability at Low Flame Lengths Percentage The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined low flame length class.
Burn Probability at Medium Flame Lengths Percentage The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined medium flame length class.
Burn Probability at High Flame Lengths Percentage The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined high flame length class.
Burn Probability at Very High Flame Lengths Percentage The likelihood that if the area represented by a pixel burns, it will burn within the user-defined very high flame length class.
Overall Burn Probability Percentage The likelihood that the area represented by a pixel will burn given a random ignition across the area of interest (for a specific set of environmental conditions). Burn probability is not an estimate of the future likelihood of a wildfire and should not be confused with empirical wildfire occurrence probabilities.

Risk Assessment by Worst Case Flame Length

Risk Assessment by Worst Case Flame Length

In this approach, risk is defined as the expected net value change within an area (in this case, an area represented by a 30 x 30 meter pixel) calculated as the product of (1) the probability that the area represented by the pixel will burn given a random ignition within the project area, and (2) the resulting change in financial or ecological value (response function) if the area represented by the pixel burns with a specific flame length.

This approach is referred to as the “worst case” estimation of fire risk because it is based on a single IFT-FlamMap run, where the areas represented by every pixel are all always assumed to burn under the worst case (i.e., by a head fire). However, for this approach, the IFT-FlamMap-RANDIG burn probability simulator provides information as to whether the area represented by a pixel will burn regardless of flame length; that is, the area represented by a pixel can be burned as a backing fire, flanking fire, or head fire in the IFT-FlamMap-RANDIG simulations. This approach may overestimate the degree of damage to the value at risk in an area represented by an individual pixel.
